# Healthcare Marketing Agency — Industry Landscape

_Deep research by Halo. 2026-02-21._

---

## The Market

Healthcare marketing is a $15B+ industry and growing. Private practices are increasingly aware they need marketing but are chronically underserved by:
1. Big agencies that don't understand healthcare
2. Tech platforms (Tebra, PatientPop) that are overpriced and cookie-cutter
3. Generalist freelancers who don't know the compliance landscape

This creates the exact gap Halo occupies: **specialized, personalized, results-driven agency at a mid-market price point.**

---

## Direct Competitors (Mapped)

### Intrepy Healthcare Marketing
- **Founded:** 2014 | Niche: Surgical specialties, MSOs, private equity-backed groups
- **Size:** Mid-size, ~20+ employees | HQ: Atlanta
- **Clients:** Orthopedics, med spas, home health, solo providers
- **Services:** SEO, paid media, website design, social media, analytics
- **Differentiator:** "We speak healthcare" — deep specialization. Has podcast (#1 healthcare marketing podcast on Apple).
- **Pricing:** Estimated $3K–$10K+/month (enterprise-focused)
- **Weakness for Halo:** Too expensive and complex for solo chiropractors or small dental practices. Long sales cycles. Not focused on fast patient acquisition.
- **Strength:** Authority, reputation, track record (575% ad conversion increase for orthopedic client)

### Ekwa Marketing
- **Founded:** 2008 | Niche: Dentists, doctors
- **Size:** ~100 employees | Model: High-volume, relationship-based
- **Services:** SEO-first, website, social, reviews
- **Pricing:** Estimated $1,500–$4,000/month
- **Weakness for Halo:** SEO-heavy (slow results). Not a paid ads shop. Old-school positioning. Clients care about visibility, not necessarily new patients fast.
- **Strength:** Long track record, 850+ practices served, thought leadership (20+ books)

### NKP Medical
- **Founded:** 2010 | Niche: Plastic surgeons, cosmetic, med spas, dental
- **Size:** Boutique | HQ: Manhattan Beach, CA
- **Services:** SEO, SEM, website, content
- **Pricing:** Premium tier ($3K–$8K+/month)
- **Weakness for Halo:** Elective/cosmetic focus, not primary care or chiropractic. High-end market only. Less relevant to Halo's niche.

### Gargle (Dental)
- **Niche:** Dental only
- **Model:** Broad dental marketing platform — SEO, social, ads, websites, postcards, reviews
- **Pricing:** $500–$2,000/month
- **Weakness for Halo:** Commodity product, low-touch, dental-only. No specialization in patient acquisition via paid ads.
- **Note:** Their onboarding quiz (website) shows they're going volume — lots of clients, not deep results

### Tebra / PatientPop (formerly separate)
- **Model:** All-in-one practice management + marketing (EHR + marketing + billing)
- **Pricing:** $300–$800/month (much cheaper, but commoditized)
- **Weakness for Halo:** Platform play, not agency. No hands-on management. Practices that use Tebra still need someone to run their ads.
- **Opportunity:** Tebra practices are IDEAL cold outreach targets — they're paying for marketing but not getting real results. Halo can come in on top.

### Cardinal Digital Marketing
- **Niche:** Multi-location provider groups, health systems, behavioral health, DSOs, PE-backed groups
- **Size:** Larger agency
- **Services:** Paid media (Google, Meta, TikTok, Display), SEO, analytics, website
- **Pricing:** Enterprise ($5K–$20K+/month)
- **Weakness for Halo:** Way too big and expensive for solo/small practices. No interest in single-location chiropractors.
- **Opportunity:** Cardinal's clients graduating down (small practices that can't afford them) or practitioners Cardinal ignores = Halo's market.

## What Competitors Are Getting Wrong

1. **Too slow:** SEO takes 6–12 months. Practitioners want patients NOW. Halo's paid ads + AI call center = results in 30–90 days.
2. **Too expensive for SMB healthcare:** $5K+/month locks out solo practitioners. Halo's $1,950 is accessible.
3. **Not full-funnel:** Most agencies drive leads but don't help practices close them. Halo wraps in automated follow-up (Bland.ai calls, SMS sequences) — the patient books, shows up, converts.
4. **No appointment-setting layer:** Competitors run ads, hand over leads, call it done. Halo's AI call center bridges the gap between "lead submitted" and "booked appointment." This is a massive competitive advantage.
5. **Generalist copy:** Competitors write generic ads. Healthcare patients respond to specificity — pain names, conditions, emotional hooks. Halo should be writing ads that could only work for a chiropractor.

## The Real Competitor: Inaction

Most practices Halo talks to aren't switching from a competitor — they're running no systematic marketing at all. They get patients from:
- Word of mouth
- Insurance networks
- Google My Business (organic, unclaimed)
- Referrals from other providers

These practices don't need to be convinced marketing works — they need to be convinced **this agency** will deliver. That's a sales problem, not a market problem. The market is wide open.

## Market Size (Rough Math)

- US chiropractors: ~70,000 active practitioners
- US dentists: ~200,000 active
- US telehealth providers: rapidly growing, ~50,000+ practices
- Physical therapists: ~300,000+
- Med spas: ~8,000+ and growing fast
- Primary care / family medicine: ~100,000+

**Addressable market for Halo (conservative):** Practices with 1–10 providers, annual revenue $500K–$5M, who would benefit from and can afford $2K/month marketing. Even 5% of chiropractors alone = 3,500 potential clients. Halo needs 150.

**This is not a market size problem.** It's a sales velocity problem.
