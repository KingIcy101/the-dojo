## Discovery Call Framework (SPIN-Based)

SPIN = Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-Payoff

The goal of the discovery call is NOT to pitch. It's to make the prospect sell themselves.

**Opening (2 min):**
"Thanks for making time. I want to make sure this conversation is useful for you regardless of what you decide — so I'm going to ask some questions about your practice first, then share what we do, and then we'll both know if it makes sense. Sound good?"

**Situation (3–5 min):**
- "How long has the practice been open?"
- "How many providers?"
- "What's your main service — what do most patients come to you for?"
- "How are you currently getting new patients? Referrals, word of mouth, ads?"
- "Roughly how many new patients are you seeing per month right now?"

**Problem (3–5 min):**
- "Is the current volume where you want it, or are you actively looking to grow?"
- "What's been your biggest challenge when it comes to getting new patients consistently?"
- "Have you tried any marketing before — ads, agencies, anything? How did that go?"
- "What would need to change for you to feel like patient acquisition isn't something you're constantly worried about?"

**Implication (2–3 min):**
(Only if they've identified a real problem — don't force it)
- "If volume stays flat for the next 12 months, what does that mean for the practice?"
- "You mentioned the last agency didn't deliver — how long did you stick with them before cutting it? What did that cost you?"
- "At [X new patients/month], you're leaving roughly [X × LTV] on the table each month. Is that math roughly right?"

**Need-Payoff (2–3 min):**
- "If we could get you consistently 15–25 new patients a month from ads, and handle all the follow-up so you're not chasing leads — what would that change for you?"
- "What would [20 new patients/month] mean in revenue for the practice over the next year?"

**Transition to pitch (only after they've confirmed the problem is real and painful):**
"Based on what you've shared, I think we can help. Can I walk you through what we do and how it works?"

## The Pitch (5 minutes max)

Keep it simple. Three things:

**1. We get you leads:**
"We run Facebook and Google ads targeted to people in your area who fit your patient profile — people with back pain, people looking for a dentist, whatever your focus is. These are real people in a 5–10 mile radius who have the problem you solve."

**2. We turn leads into bookings:**
"This is where most agencies fall short. When someone submits a form, we automatically text them within 60 seconds and have our scheduling team call within 5 minutes. We follow up 5–7 times over the following week. Most of your competitors don't call for hours — we're already on the phone."

**3. You see the numbers:**
"Every month you get a report: leads generated, appointments booked, show rate, revenue attributed. You always know what you're getting for your investment."

## Handling Objections

### "How much does it cost?"
→ Never lead with price. Only give price after they've confirmed the problem is real.
→ "Our management fee is $1,950/month, and you'd set aside a separate budget for ad spend — typically $1,000–$2,000/month to start. So all-in, around $3,000–$4,000/month. That said, most of our clients see a positive ROI within 60–90 days. Want me to walk through the math on what 15 new patients would mean for your practice?"

### "That's too expensive."
→ "I hear you. Let me ask — what would you expect to invest to get 15–20 new patients per month? ... [Let them answer] ... The math on our end typically looks like: [X leads] at $[CPL] = [Y bookings] at an average value of $[LTV]. For most practices that's a 3–5x return. Does the ROI change how you're thinking about it?"

### "We tried ads before and they didn't work."
→ "That's the most common thing I hear. Mind if I ask what you were running? ... [Listen] ... The difference is usually two things: targeting (most agencies target too broadly) and follow-up (most agencies don't have one — leads go cold). Both of those are where we put the most attention. Would you be open to hearing specifically what we'd do differently?"

### "I need to think about it."
→ "Of course. Can I ask — is there something specific that's unclear, or is it more about timing?"
→ If unclear: Clarify and address.
→ If timing: "What would need to change for it to be the right time?"
→ Set a specific follow-up: "I'll follow up Thursday — does 10am or 2pm work better?"

### "I need to talk to my business partner / spouse."
→ "Absolutely — that makes sense. Would it be helpful to have a second call with both of you? I can walk them through the same overview so you're both working from the same info."

### "Can you guarantee results?"
→ "We don't guarantee specific numbers — no honest agency can, because ad performance depends on your market, your offer, and a few factors outside our control. What I can tell you is: we've never had a client not get leads from Facebook and Google. The variable is conversion rate — and that's why we put so much into the follow-up system. We also offer a 30-day check-in — if we're not on track by then, we'll tell you before you do."

## Closing the Deal

Most people overthink the close. It's just asking.

After handling objections, the close is:

**"Based on everything we've talked about, do you want to move forward?"**

Or softer: **"Does this feel like the right fit for where you're trying to take the practice?"**

If yes → immediately:
- "Great. I'll send over the contract today — it's a simple one-page agreement. Once that's signed and the first month's payment is processed, we start the onboarding call within 48 hours and have ads live within 5 business days."

**Don't let the deal die in "send me the contract" limbo:**
- Send within the hour
- Follow up within 24 hours if not signed
- Contract should be digital (DocuSign or HelloSign) — frictionless

## Pricing Psychology

### Why $1,950 is the right price (not $1,500, not $2,500 to start)

- $1,950 is odd — feels more considered than $2,000
- Under $2K keeps it below the "committee decision" threshold for most solo practitioners
- High enough to be respected (below $1,500 = "cheap agency" in their mind)
- Easy upgrade path: $2,950 when adding platforms or specializing further

### Anchoring in the sales conversation

Before giving your price, anchor up:
"Some of the larger healthcare marketing agencies charge $5,000–$10,000/month for what we do. We've built the same system at a fraction of the cost because we focus exclusively on practices like yours."

Now $1,950 feels like a deal.

### Monthly vs. annual

Offer monthly to start (removes friction). At month 3–4 when they're happy, offer annual:
"If you want to lock in this rate for 12 months, I can give you a small discount — effectively one month free. A lot of our clients do this because they know the system is working."

Annual contracts dramatically improve LTV and churn protection.

## The Follow-Up System (Post Discovery Call)

Most deals don't close on the first call. They close on the 3rd–5th touchpoint.

| Day | Action |
|---|---|
| Same day | Send recap email: "Here's what we discussed + contract link" |
| Day 2 | Check-in if contract not signed: "Any questions before you review?" |
| Day 5 | Follow-up call: "I wanted to make sure you got the contract — any hesitations I can address?" |
| Day 10 | Case study email: Send a relevant result ("I wanted to share what we did for a chiropractor in [similar state]") |
| Day 15 | Final reach: "I have a slot opening up for [their city area] — wanted to give you first right of refusal" |
| Day 30 | Re-engage: "Checking back in — is patient growth still on your radar?" |
